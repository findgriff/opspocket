Most marketing teams context-switch constantly — jumping between research, writing, technical setup, and performance review. This pipeline separates each of those concerns into a dedicated agent with a specific mandate. No one agent does everything. Together, they do it all.

Why a Pipeline Instead of One Agent?

Giving a single agent the instruction "run a full marketing campaign" produces mediocre results. The output reflects the average of everything rather than the depth of anything. A specialist pipeline works differently: each agent brings focused reasoning to its narrow domain, and hands off structured, decision-quality outputs to the next stage.

Atlas does not write copy. Foundry does not do research. Stack does not invent strategy. The boundaries keep each agent sharp — and the handoff chain keeps the whole thing coherent.

The Agents

🔭
Atlas
Market Intelligence Agent

Atlas goes first. Before any message is written or any asset is produced, Atlas maps the landscape — who the audience actually is, what they urgently care about, where competitors are weak, and which campaign angles have the strongest underlying demand.

Its outputs aren't opinions. They're structured research: audience profiles, pain point matrices, competitor observations, and specific campaign angles with reasoning. Every downstream agent builds on what Atlas produces.

Audience Research ICP Mapping Competitor Analysis Trend Spotting Campaign Angles Objection Discovery
📡
Echo
Positioning & Messaging Agent

Echo takes Atlas's research and translates it into the language a campaign actually needs. It defines what the offer should say, what emotions it should trigger, and which specific words will resonate with which audience segments.

The output isn't a finished ad — it's a messaging architecture. Value propositions, positioning statements, hook options, CTA variants, and objection-handling language that Foundry then uses to produce actual assets.

Value Propositions Message Pillars Hook Writing CTA Development Offer Framing Persona Variants
⚒️
Foundry
Creative Production Agent

Foundry is where strategy becomes deliverables. Given approved positioning and campaign goals, it produces the actual materials: email sequences, ad copy, landing page copy, social posts, scripts, content calendars, and creative briefs ready for execution.

Foundry doesn't invent strategy — it executes against what's already been decided upstream. That constraint keeps output focused and consistent. When gaps exist, Foundry flags them rather than filling them with guesswork.

Email Copy Ad Creative Landing Pages Social Posts Content Calendars A/B Variants
🔧
Stack
Marketing Systems & Full-Stack Agent

Stack owns the technical layer. Landing pages, forms, CRM connections, analytics events, UTM structures, lead routing logic, automation setup — everything that turns marketing concepts into working systems that actually capture and convert.

Its output is operational infrastructure: implementation plans, tracking specs, CRM field mappings, QA checklists, and deployment steps. Stack thinks like a marketing engineer — reliability and attribution first, speed second.

Landing Pages CRM Integration Event Tracking Lead Routing Automation A/B Infrastructure
📈
Catalyst
Funnel Optimisation Agent

Catalyst activates after launch. It reads performance data — CTR, CPL, conversion rates, CAC, engagement patterns — and turns numbers into decisions. Where are leads dropping off? What's draining budget without return? Which content type is outperforming everything else?

Critically, Catalyst closes the loop. Its findings feed back to Atlas when audience assumptions are wrong, to Echo when messaging is underperforming, to Foundry when creative formats need rethinking, and to Stack when tracking or conversion infrastructure is broken.

Pipeline in Action

EXAMPLE · Facebook Page campaign for a service business

A

Atlas — Understand the audience

Given the brief: "grow a Facebook Page for a local bookkeeping firm and generate leads from small business owners." Atlas researches audience segments, maps common pain points (cash flow anxiety, tax deadlines, fear of being caught out), scans competitor page activity, and identifies which content themes typically drive engagement in this niche.

→ Audience: SMB owners, 35–55, 1–10 employees, revenue-conscious
→ Top pain points: tax deadlines, late invoices, unclear cash position
→ Content opportunities: myth-busting, deadline reminders, "what your numbers mean" education
→ Lead magnet angle: "5 bookkeeping mistakes costing small businesses money"
E

Echo — Build the messaging framework

Working from Atlas's research, Echo defines the page voice (calm authority, not salesy), builds three message pillars, writes a CTA library, and produces hook variants for educational and promotional posts.

→ Voice: "your numbers, made simple" — clear, reassuring, no jargon
→ Pillar 1: Education (what the numbers mean)
→ Pillar 2: Peace of mind (we handle it, you focus on growth)
→ Pillar 3: Social proof (real client outcomes)
→ Hero hook: "Most small businesses overpay tax. Here's why."
→ Lead magnet CTA: "Get the free checklist →"
F

Foundry — Create two weeks of content

Using Echo's messaging architecture, Foundry produces a two-week publishing calendar: 10 posts across the five content buckets, caption variants for A/B testing, a promo post for the lead magnet, and community engagement prompts.

→ 4 educational posts (myth-busting, explainer format)
→ 2 trust posts (behind-the-scenes, credentials)
→ 2 engagement posts (polls, questions)
→ 1 testimonial post
→ 1 promotional post (lead magnet offer)
→ 2 caption variants per post for testing
S

Stack — Wire up the lead capture funnel

Foundry's lead magnet promo links to a landing page. Stack builds the page structure, defines the form fields, maps leads into the CRM, sets up UTM parameters for Facebook attribution, configures the follow-up automation, and writes the QA checklist before go-live.

→ Landing page: headline / 3 bullets / single-field email form
→ UTM: utm_source=facebook&utm_campaign=bookkeeping-checklist
→ CRM field mapping: email, source, lead_date, campaign_name
→ Automation: welcome email + 3-day nurture sequence triggered on form submit
→ QA: form submit → CRM entry → welcome email — all confirmed ✓
C

Catalyst — Measure, diagnose, improve

After two weeks live, Catalyst reviews page reach, post engagement by type, landing page conversion rate, lead quality in CRM, and email open rates. It identifies what's working, what isn't, and produces a prioritised test backlog for the next cycle.

→ Best performing: myth-busting educational posts (3× avg reach)
→ Underperforming: behind-the-scenes posts (low saves, low CTR)
→ Funnel finding: landing page converting at 18% — strong, maintain copy
→ Bottleneck: 60% email open rate drops to 12% by day 3 of nurture
→ Next test: rewrite day-3 email subject line (send to Echo)
→ Atlas feedback: add "seasonal tax deadline" content pillar

Triggering the Pipeline

The pipeline can be kicked off with a single instruction to the Orchestrator — the agent that coordinates all five specialists and manages handoffs between them. A typical trigger looks like this:

# Example pipeline trigger
You → Orchestrator

Run the full pipeline for a Facebook Page campaign.
Business: local bookkeeping firm targeting small business owners.
Goal: grow page following and capture leads with a free tax checklist.
Start with Atlas and work through to Foundry. Flag if you need anything
before Stack and Catalyst can activate.

The Orchestrator routes the brief to Atlas, collects structured outputs at each stage, passes them forward with clear handoff summaries, and flags any blockers before moving to the next agent. You can watch the whole pipeline run through Mission Control, or let it complete autonomously and review the final outputs.

What Each Agent Hands Off

Structured handoffs are what separate a real pipeline from a series of disconnected prompts. Each agent produces outputs in a format the next agent can immediately use:

  • Atlas → Echo: Audience profile, pain point matrix, content opportunity map, recommended angles
  • Echo → Foundry: Messaging framework, pillars, hook library, CTA library, persona variants
  • Foundry → Stack: Asset requirements, landing page copy structure, CTA destinations, tracking requirements
  • Stack → Catalyst: Live URLs, tracking confirmation, CRM mapping, attribution notes
  • Catalyst → Upstream: Performance diagnosis, test recommendations, feedback to Atlas, Echo, Foundry, or Stack as appropriate

No agent invents what the previous stage should have provided. If a handoff is incomplete, the receiving agent flags what's missing rather than guessing. That discipline keeps the whole system reliable across multiple campaign cycles.
